# Game Design Intelligence Rubrics

## Core Loop

- Fantasy: the player can say who they are and what they do.
- Verb clarity: primary actions are legible and expressive.
- Feedback: consequences are readable and timely.
- Reinvestment: each loop changes future choices.
- Skill gradient: skilled players make different decisions than novices.
- Variety: repetition changes through context, mastery, risk, or social meaning.
- Failure: mistakes teach and preserve momentum.

## Systems And Emergence

- Rules are simple enough to understand and rich enough to combine.
- Systems affect each other through stable affordances.
- Emergent outcomes are surprising but explainable.
- Constraints prevent dominant exploits.
- The design has testable hypotheses about player creativity.

## Progression

- Progression changes decisions, not only numbers.
- Skill, content, power, status, and identity layers are intentionally mixed.
- Early progression builds confidence.
- Mid progression expands mastery.
- Late progression adds depth without endless chores.
- Catch-up protects latecomers and lapsed players.

## Economy

- Every resource has a source, sink, purpose, and player meaning.
- Scarcity creates choice, not paralysis.
- Abundance creates agency, not inflation.
- Conversion paths are understandable.
- Premium or paid paths do not short-circuit the core loop.
- Segment stress is checked across novice, expert, casual, latecomer, lapsed, free, and spender players.

## Balance

- Multiple strategies are viable.
- Tradeoffs are readable.
- Counters exist and are discoverable.
- No dominant strategy wins across most contexts.
- Options are not so equal that choice becomes meaningless.
- Tuning has a stated power curve, test range, and playtest probe.

## Motivation And Retention

- Autonomy: players choose meaningful goals or paths.
- Competence: players can see improvement.
- Relatedness: social systems support belonging or respectful rivalry.
- Curiosity: the game leaves room to discover.
- Emotion: the design supports more than one kind of fun.
- Return value: coming back creates fresh value, not guilt.
- Commercial pattern fits the fantasy and does not undermine trust.

## Onboarding And Difficulty

- First action teaches the fantasy.
- Each new rule is introduced safely, then required, then recombined.
- Friction points are visible and testable.
- Failure cost matches learning value.
- Challenge paths support different skill bands.
- Assist options preserve dignity and clarity.

## Multiplayer And Social Health

- Player-to-player actions and incentives are mapped.
- The design rewards cooperation, teaching, leadership, and repair when relevant.
- Competition supports mastery rather than humiliation.
- Communication tools are designed with abuse risk in mind.
- Newcomers, solo players, teams, veterans, and spectators have clear roles.
- Moderation is not the only defense against toxic dynamics.

## Live Service And Content

- The daily or weekly loop is worth repeating without becoming a chore.
- Cadence matches team capacity.
- Events have a clear fantasy and mechanical reason.
- Battle pass tasks allow playstyle choice and catch-up.
- Late-game expands decision space, not just grind.
- Quality-of-life updates reduce friction without erasing mastery.

## Ethical Guardrails

Flag designs that rely on fake scarcity, opaque odds, pay-to-win, artificial deficit, coercive streaks, sunk-cost pressure, forced appointments, hostile comparison, or dark-pattern friction. Reframe toward transparent value, optional goals, fair challenge, catch-up, player-set pace, and respectful social systems.
